# Runbound v0.9.69 — XDP perf ceiling on 10 GbE

**Date:** 2026-06-03
**Mode:** Bare-metal AF_XDP zero-copy (Drv), Intel X520 (82599) 10 GbE
**Tool:** [dnsmark](https://github.com/redlemonbe/dnsmark) ≥ 1.2.1

---

## Result

Local-zone A answer path (`a.bench.test A`), measured **at the receiver NIC counters** under a saturating 10 GbE flood:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| RX drained (responses processed) | **8.83M qps** |
| Receiver CPU (16 RSS cores) | 53% |
| NIC | Intel X520 / 82599, 16 RSS queues |
| Receiver host | **dual Xeon E5-2690 v2 (Ivy Bridge, 2013)** |
| 10 GbE fibre line-rate (~111 B responses) | ~11.3M qps |
| **Fraction of line-rate** | **~78%** |

8.83M qps on the local-zone fast path, on a 13-year-old CPU at half utilisation. The generator (dnsmark on a Threadripper 5995WX) saturates the link at ~11.9M pps — the 10 GbE fibre is the limit, not the silicon.

---

## How it was measured (and a warning)

**Do not trust the round-trip throughput reported by the load generator when the receiver out-paces the generator's own receive path.** dnsmark's XSK RX caps around 1–2M and under-counts badly: it reported ~1M while the receiver was really processing 8.3M. Measure the receiver's true rate at its NIC counters (`ethtool -S <nic>`):

- **RX drained** = Σ `rx_queue_N_packets` (delta over the window)
- **TX responses** = `tx_bytes_nic` delta ÷ response size. Note: AF_XDP zero-copy TX **bypasses the standard `tx_packets`/`tx_bytes`** counters (they stay 0); only `tx_bytes_nic` reflects ZC TX.
- **Dropped at NIC** = `rx_missed_errors` delta (NIC received but the host didn't supply free descriptors fast enough)
- `offered ≈ drained + missed`

CPU saturation via `mpstat -P <cores> 1 5`.

## Tuning (both ends)

- `ethtool -A <nic> rx off tx off` — disable 802.3x PAUSE (flow control throttles hard otherwise).
- `ethtool -N <nic> rx-flow-hash udp4 sdfn` — hash UDP on L4 ports so RSS spreads across queues.
- `ethtool -X <nic> equal 16` — the 82599 RSS RETA caps at 16 queues regardless of core count.
- `ethtool -G <nic> rx 4096 tx 4096` — max ring sizes.
- CPU governor `performance` + a 15 s warm-up flood — `schedutil`/`powersave` under-clock the bursty DNS hot path mid-flood (the same binary measured 5.9M → 7.2M depending on DVFS state).

## Honest findings

- **Not CPU-bound at 8.83M** — 16 RSS cores at 53%, 0/16 above 90%. Per-core CPU optimisations therefore do **not** raise single-link 10 GbE throughput.
- **The incremental IPv4 checksum (v0.9.69) removes ~20% of per-packet CPU** (`ipv4_checksum` was the #2 hotspot) but, since the path is not CPU-bound on 10 GbE, single-link peak throughput is unchanged. It is a latency/headroom win that pays off on CPU-bound or 25 GbE+ deployments.
- **`xdp-busy-poll` (drain-first) does not raise peak throughput** — under saturation `poll()` returns immediately, so drain-first and poll-first are equivalent. It only reduces idle/first-packet latency.
- The remaining gap to line-rate (~4.7M `rx_missed` with idle CPU) is **RX-buffer / fill-ring / NIC-side**, not CPU and not the poll model. Not pursued: on 10 GbE we are already near the fibre limit; beyond ~11M needs 25/40/100 GbE, not code.

**Methodology lesson:** trust the receiver's NIC counters and medians, never the generator's round-trip number or peaks; and verify a suspected bottleneck before changing code.
